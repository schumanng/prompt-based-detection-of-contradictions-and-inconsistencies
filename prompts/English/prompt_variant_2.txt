From now on, please act as a classifier who has to decide for each pair of given sentences (hereinafter referred to as 'sentence pair') whether they contradict each other, are neutral to each other, are inconsistent, or have different contexts (so you have exactly four possible answers: 'Contradiction', 'Neutral', 'Inconsistent', and 'Not related').
The following describes the conditions under which each of the four target classes applies to a given sentence pair.
    
target class: 'contradiction': 
In principle, a sentence pair is considered a 'contradiction' if the normative statements described in the two sentences cannot be true at the same time or cannot be followed at the same time, i.e., only either 'sentence 1' or 'sentence 2' is possible. The following rules are intended to assist in the classification of the pairs of sentences submitted: 
   - Antonyms, negations, and mitigations: A contradiction exists when one of the two sentences reverses or mitigates the normative statement of the other sentence through antonyms, negations, or other terms, so that both statements can no longer be followed simultaneously. In addition to verbs (often modal verbs) or adjectives, adverbs and nouns can also lead to a contradiction between two normative statements. Important: Mitigating factors can also be indicated by terms such as 'if applicable', 'where applicable', 'where possible', 'as far as possible', 'if possible', etc., provided they only appear in one of the two sentences and refer to the relevant normative statement. Also important: the verbs 'shall' / 'should' and 'must' are NOT antonyms (i.e., they do not automatically lead to a contradiction). Similarly, the word combinations 'shall not' and 'must not' are NOT antonyms.
   - Specifications without alternatives: A contradiction exists when one of the sentences describes a normative statement as the only possible option for a particular situation, condition, or group of addressees, while the other sentence provides several alternatives for it. In the case of permissions, such contradictions are typically indicated by strict adjectives and adverbs such as 'only', 'exclusively', etc., and apply even if only one of the two sentences contains them. In the case of obligations, however, they also apply without strict adjectives.
   - Same specification, more extensive conditions: A contradiction exists if both sentences have a semantically identical normative statement, but one of the sentences attaches more extensive (preliminary) conditions to it than the other sentence.
   - Contradictory numerical object characterization: A contradiction exists when both sentences have the same subject or the same condition, but the numerical values that characterize the (also identical) objects concerned are different in both sentences.
   - Contradictory numerical subject characterization: A contradiction exists when both sentences have a semantically identical normative statement, but the numerical values that characterize the (also identical) subjects of the sentences are different.
   - Deviating (individual) objects: A contradiction exists if different objects are referenced or affected for exactly the same situation/condition, so that the respective normative requirements cannot be complied with at the same time.
    
target class: 'Inconsistent': 
Basically, a sentence pair is considered 'inconsistent' if a comparison of the two sentences makes sense because they deal with the same topic (the same context), but their essential normative statements are inconsistent without directly contradicting each other. This means that both statements/regulations could be followed at the same time without the other making this impossible. Nevertheless, a discrepancy remains that is relevant from a regulatory perspective. The target class 'inconsistent' thus represents a preliminary stage of contradiction and can be interpreted and classified on the basis of the following three identifying features: 
   - Additional situations or addressees: An inconsistency exists if one of the two sentences specifies more situations or addressees for the same action than the other sentence. If the sentence does not contain any strict terms that lead to a restriction (e.g., “the action only applies in situation XY” or only to addressee group Z), then these statements do not contradict each other, but differ in the scope of their application.
   - Same situation, more detailed information: An inconsistency exists if one of the two sentences contains a more precise instruction for the same situation/condition and thus leaves the person concerned less leeway in following the respective instruction.
   - Deviating partial information: If both statements do indeed overlap in terms of identical information/requirements, but a discrepancy remains that may affect compliance with the respective requirement (i.e., the information is not more precise—as in the condition “same facts, more detailed information”—but deviates in certain aspects).
   - Part-whole relationship through generic terms: An inconsistency exists when both sentences are directed at a specific addressee or a specific situation (subject), but one of the sentences uses a generic term for the other subject or encompasses all subtypes of the other subject.
    
target class: 'Neutral': 
Basically, a sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if a comparison of the two sentences makes sense because they deal with the same topic (the same context) and are neither contradictory nor inconsistent. The target class 'neutral' can be interpreted and classified using the following distinguishing features (important: there are a few exceptions in this category, which are also described below):
   - Neutral words or phrases: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences contains additional neutral words or phrases that do not appear in the other sentence. However, it is crucial that these words or phrases do not have a restrictive influence on the interpretation or compliance with the respective normative statements, or extend the statements or specifications in such a way that this would result in an inconsistency with the other sentence. In other words, it is possible that a single deviating word can create an inconsistency if it does not appear in the other sentence and thus actually noticeably changes or expands the requirement (e.g., if the word 'in writing' appears in only one of the two sentences). It is therefore important to note that in case of doubt (i.e., if the significance of the supposedly neutral word or phrase is difficult to assess), it is better to choose the target class 'Inconsistent'.
   - Independent normative subordinate clauses: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences contains a subordinate clause that makes an additional normative statement, but one that does not influence, further specify, or concretize the main statement (which the other sentence also addresses), and can instead be considered separately from it. This is typically recognizable by the presence of an additional situation or subject in the sentence (often in combination with another verb). Such independent normative subordinate clauses can appear at the beginning or the end of the main statement.
   - Use of (additional) examples: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences enriches a normative statement with additional examples, while the other sentence contains no or fewer examples. Such cases must be classified as 'neutral' because the normative statement itself is not changed, but merely explained in more detail by examples of the respective entities/objects.
   - Mentioning an option as an example: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences mentions several possibilities/options for dealing with a situation and the other sentence uses one of these options as an example. However, if one of the options is mentioned not as an example but as a fixed instruction, the sentence pair must be classified as a 'contradiction'.
   - Synonyms and abbreviations: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences uses clear synonyms or abbreviations for terms in the other sentence. Important: This rule does not apply to generic terms! Also important: If you are unsure about a potential synonym or abbreviation, it is better to choose the target class 'Inconsistent'
   - Justifications vs. no justifications: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences provides additional justification or rationale for a normative statement, but the other does not. Such cases can be interpreted as 'neutral' because this additional information is not decisive for compliance with the respective regulation.
   - Differing justifications: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if both sentences provide a justification, but these justifications differ. The assumption: As long as the actual normative statement is identical, the justification should be of secondary importance.
   - Potential and actual conditions: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences attaches a (pre)condition to a specific normative statement that is already considered fulfilled in the other sentence.
   - Universal statements: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences makes a normative statement directed at a specific addressee (subject) or a specific situation, while the other sentence expresses the same statement in a completely universal manner, such that it applies to all addressees or situations. In any case, however, it is crucial that the sentence which specifically names the addressees/situations does not do so in a strict/restrictive manner (e.g., “Only employees must…”), as this would otherwise contradict the universal statement of the other sentence.
   - Support for locating objects and entities: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if only one of the two sentences specifies where the referenced subjects, objects, or other entities are located in the real world. However, such cases should only be marked as neutral if this information does not alter the respective normative statement but merely provides the reader with additional guidance.
   - Contrasting subject and contrasting object: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if the two sentences have contrasting subject phrases, but the respective object phrases are also contrasting and are therefore 'neutral' in relation to each other. Keep in mind with this rule that such cases are most likely very rare.
   - Divergent cross-references: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if both sentences refer to different places where the (identical) normative statements are found in the respective regulatory document or where more detailed information can be found. This rule is based on our basic assumption that the two sentences provided come from different regulatory documents. This inevitably leads to cases where the sentences refer to different places within their respective documents, simply because the documents may be structured differently. However, as long as the essential normative statement between the two sentences is semantically identical and can stand completely on its own without the respective cross-reference, these cases should be classified as 'neutral'.
    
target class: 'Not related': 
A sentence pair must be classified as 'Not related' if a comparison of the two sentences does not make sense because they do not deal with the same topic. This does not mean that the two sentences are unrelated, but rather that the inherent essential normative statement within the overarching topic is different between the two sentences and therefore they are not related to each other. The target class 'Not related' can be interpreted and classified based on the following distinguishing features:
   - Different subject characteristics: A sentence pair is considered to be a 'Not related' if the essential normative statement (e.g., a requirement/measure/obligation, etc.) is identical in both sentences, but the only subject (situation, entity, addressee, condition, etc.) to which the normative statement refers differs in certain aspects. These cases occur frequently in the regulatory context and especially when comparing different documents, but should not automatically lead to contradiction or inconsistency messages, as both normative statements can refer to several subjects or situations at the same time, i.e., they can apply simultaneously as long as they do not contradict each other.
   - (Single) differing verb and no object: A sentence pair is considered to be in a 'Not related' if there is no object in the sentence and the subject or condition is the same in both sentences, but the (only occurring) verb that affects it is different. Since the object is missing, the verb stands as the 'only action' for the respective context in which the sentence is to be interpreted. If these verbs are different, the two sentences are no longer in the same context and can therefore apply simultaneously, i.e., they can be valid at the same time.
   - Different contextual nuances: A sentence pair is considered to be 'Not related' if the subject (or situation) and an action relating to the subject are identical, but the context connecting them is different. This would be the case, for example, if two sentences make normative statements about the collection and processing of personal data, but one sentence refers to the 'what for' and the other sentence to the 'how'.
   - Uncertainty about co-reference: When one subject may be a specified form of the other subject, but this cannot be said with certainty (without knowing the surrounding context).
    
Important: When making your classification, please take all of the rules described into account at the same time, i.e., always ensure that no other rules or conditions are violated. Feel free to take some time to think about your decision.
Please only give the name of the respective target class as your answer (i.e., either 'Contradiction,' 'Inconsistent,' 'Neutral,' or 'Not related')!
