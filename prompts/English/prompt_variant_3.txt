From now on, please act as a classifier who has to decide for each pair of given sentences (hereinafter referred to as 'sentence pair') whether they contradict each other, are neutral to each other, are inconsistent, or have different contexts (so you have exactly four possible answers: 'Contradiction', 'Neutral', 'Inconsistent', and 'Not related').
The following describes the conditions under which each of the four target classes applies to a given sentence pair.
    
target class: 'contradiction': 
In principle, a sentence pair is considered a 'contradiction' if the normative statements described in the two sentences cannot be true at the same time or cannot be followed at the same time, i.e., only either 'sentence 1' or 'sentence 2' is possible. The following rules are intended to assist in the classification of the pairs of sentences submitted: 
   - Antonyms, negations, and mitigations: A contradiction exists when one of the two sentences reverses or mitigates the normative statement of the other sentence through antonyms, negations, or other terms, so that both statements can no longer be followed simultaneously (e.g., sentence 1: 'If necessary, the supplier can issue an order confirmation.', Sentence 2: 'The supplier must issue an order confirmation in all cases.'). In addition to verbs (often modal verbs) or adjectives, adverbs and nouns can also lead to a contradiction between two normative statements. An example of a weakening would be: Sentence 1: 'All welding and burning work must be separated from other activities in terms of space and time.', Sentence 2: 'Welding and burning work should be separated from other activities in terms of space and time wherever possible. (Important: Mitigating factors can also be indicated by terms such as 'if applicable', 'where applicable', 'where possible', 'as far as possible', 'if possible', etc., provided they only appear in one of the two sentences and refer to the relevant normative statement. Example: Sentence 1: 'The result of the audit will be communicated to the supplier in the form of a written audit report, if applicable. ', Sentence 2: 'The result of the audit will be communicated to the supplier in the form of a written audit report. Also important: the verbs 'shall' / 'should' and 'must' are NOT antonyms (i.e., they do not automatically lead to a contradiction). Similarly, the word combinations 'shall not' and 'must not' are NOT antonyms (the following sentence pair would therefore NOT be a contradiction: Sentence 1: 'Private items that are not required for work must not be brought into the workplace.', Sentence 2: 'Private items that are not required for work shall not be brought into the workplace.').
   - Specifications without alternatives: A contradiction exists when one of the sentences describes a normative statement as the only possible option for a particular situation, condition, or group of addressees, while the other sentence provides several alternatives for it. In the case of permissions, such contradictions are typically indicated by strict adjectives and adverbs such as 'only', 'exclusively', etc., and apply even if only one of the two sentences contains them (e.g., sentence 1: 'Employees of partner companies may park their cars exclusively in the parking garage to the left of the main gate.', Sentence 2: 'Employees of partner companies may park their cars in the parking garage to the left of the main gate or in the outdoor parking lot to the right of the driveway.'). In the case of obligations, however, they also apply without strict adjectives (e.g., Sentence 1: 'All rules must be included in the operating manual.', Sentence 2: 'All rules must be included in the operating manual or in the Betra.') or if it is only implied that there are alternatives to the prescribed instruction (e.g. Sentence 1: 'The technical briefing on site must be documented in the written work permit.', Sentence 2: 'The technical briefing on site must be documented (e.g. in the written work permit).').
   - Same specification, more extensive conditions: A contradiction exists if both sentences have a semantically identical normative statement, but one of the sentences attaches more extensive (preliminary) conditions to it than the other sentence (e.g., sentence 1: 'The components are approved for installation provided that no defects are found during the identification test.', Sentence 2: 'If no defects are found during the identification test and the control tests, the components are approved for installation.').
   - Contradictory numerical object characterization: A contradiction exists when both sentences have the same subject or the same condition, but the numerical values that characterize the (also identical) objects concerned are different in both sentences (e.g., sentence 1: 'Invalid specification documents are subject to a retention period of at least 2 years.', Sentence 2: 'Invalid specification documents must be retained for at least 1.5 years.').
   - Contradictory numerical subject characterization: A contradiction exists when both sentences have a semantically identical normative statement, but the numerical values that characterize the (also identical) subjects of the sentences are different (e.g., sentence 1: 'If the defect rate is less than 3%, consultation with the supplier will take place to determine whether this damage will also be treated as serial damage.' Sentence 2: 'If the defect rate is < 2.5%, consultation with the supplier will take place to clarify whether this damage also qualifies as serial damage.'). Such statements cannot coexist because they do not address different variants of the same subject, but rather exactly the same subjects, which are conditioned differently by the numerical values.
   - Deviating (individual) objects: A contradiction exists if different objects are referenced or affected for exactly the same situation/condition, so that the respective normative requirements cannot be complied with at the same time (e.g., sentence 1: “All cases of violations of the regulations for the protection of personal data must be reported immediately to the data protection coordinator.” sentence 2: 'If violations of the regulations on the protection of personal data become known, these must be reported immediately to the management.').
    
target class: 'Inconsistent': 
Basically, a sentence pair is considered 'inconsistent' if a comparison of the two sentences makes sense because they deal with the same topic (the same context), but their essential normative statements are inconsistent without directly contradicting each other. This means that both statements/regulations could be followed at the same time without the other making this impossible. Nevertheless, a discrepancy remains that is relevant from a regulatory perspective. The target class 'inconsistent' thus represents a preliminary stage of contradiction and can be interpreted and classified on the basis of the following two identifying features: 
   - Additional situations or addressees: An inconsistency exists if one of the two sentences specifies more situations or addressees for the same action than the other sentence. If the sentence does not contain any strict terms that lead to a restriction (e.g., “the action only applies in situation XY” or only to addressee group Z), then these statements do not contradict each other, but differ in the scope of their application. An example of an inconsistent sentence pair due to “additional situations or addressees” is: Sentence 1: 'When handling flammable liquids, avoid all sources of ignition.' Sentence 2: 'When handling flammable liquids and in rooms at risk of explosion, avoid sources of ignition.' Another example would be: Sentence 1: 'In the event of a noticeable odor, dust, discomfort, an alarm, or particular difficulties, work must be stopped immediately.' Sentence 2: 'Work must be stopped immediately if there is a noticeable odor, discomfort, in the event of an alarm, or in the event of particular difficulties.' Third example: Sentence 1: 'Hearing protection must be worn in designated noise areas.' Sentence 2: 'Hearing protection must be worn in designated noise areas or during noisy activities.'
   - Same situation, more detailed information: An inconsistency exists if one of the two sentences contains a more precise instruction for the same situation/condition and thus leaves the person concerned less leeway in following the respective instruction (example 1: Sentence 1: 'If a non-conformity is detected in a delivery item prior to delivery, a concession must be requested from the client before the planned delivery.' Sentence 2: 'If a non-conformity is detected in a delivery item prior to delivery, a concession must be requested from the client in writing at least 4 working days before the planned delivery.' Example 2: Sentence 1: 'The shut-off valve must be designed to withstand a pressure corresponding to 1.5 times the working pressure (MPa) without leaking or deforming.' Sentence 2: 'The shut-off valve must remain tight (to the outside) at a pressure corresponding to 1.5 times the working pressure (MPa) (see Appendix 5B).' Example 3: Sentence 1: 'Load carriers must be returned to the cycle in a clean, dry, and undamaged condition.' Sentence 2: 'Load carriers must be returned to the cycle in a clean, dust-free, dry, and undamaged condition.' Example 4: Sentence 1: 'Vehicles may only be parked in designated/allocated parking spaces on the factory premises after consultation with the person responsible for the order at Mustermann AG. Sentence 2: 'Vehicles may only be parked in designated parking spaces on company premises.' Example 5: Sentence 1: 'Mixed loading units (mixed pallets) should be kept to a minimum.' Sentence 2: 'Mixed loading units (mixed pallets) should be kept to a minimum or avoided altogether, especially in the case of KLTs.' Example 6: Sentence 1: 'No employee may use their position to demand, accept, obtain, or promise advantages.' Sentence 2: 'No employee may demand or accept improper advantages.' Example 7: Sentence 1: 'For items with variable weights, an EAN-13 barcode with prefix 27 (with encrypted weight) must be affixed.' Sentence 2: 'For items with variable weights, a barcode with prefix 27 (with encrypted weight) must be affixed.' Example 8: Sentence 1: 'The truck must be secured against rolling away by means of wheel chocks.' Sentence 2: 'The truck must be secured against rolling away.' Example 9: Sentence 1: 'The supplier is obliged to comply with the respective legal regulations regarding the treatment of employees, environmental protection, and occupational safety.' Sentence 2: 'The supplier guarantees/undertakes to comply with all legal regulations relating to environmental protection and occupational safety.' Important: An inconsistency also exists if relevant additional information is provided in parentheses (however, it is important that this is not preceded by 'e.g.' or 'for example' and that only one of the two sentences mentions this relevant additional information: Example 10: Sentence 1: 'The user must check the operational safety of the work equipment used every working day.', Sentence 2: 'The user must check the operational safety of the work equipment used every working day (by visual inspection before starting work).' Example 11: Sentence 1: 'The storage and use of hazardous substances must be reported to the coordinator in advance.' Sentence 2: 'The storage and use of hazardous substances must be reported to the coordinator in advance (by means of a safety data sheet).'
   - Deviating partial information: If both statements do indeed overlap in terms of identical information/requirements, but a discrepancy remains that may affect compliance with the respective requirement (i.e., the information is not more precise—as in the condition “same facts, more detailed information”—but deviates in certain aspects). Example: Sentence 1: 'Every employee shall immediately report any violations of this data protection policy or other regulations for the protection of personal data to their respective supervisor, data protection coordinator, or data protection officer. Sentence 2: 'Every employee shall immediately report any violations of this data protection policy or other regulations for the protection of personal data (data protection incidents) to their respective supervisor, the management, or the data protection officer. In many cases, there is a deviation in partial information (and thus an inconsistency), particularly when certain terms differ in a list within the two sentences or when one of the two sentences lists more terms than the other (Example 1: Sentence 1: 'Discrimination is prohibited, in particular on the grounds of a person's ethnic origin, religion and belief, disability, age, sexual identity, or gender.' Sentence 2: 'No discrimination on the basis of age, gender, religion, national or ethnic origin, marital status, disability, culture, political opinion, sexual orientation, or social affiliation will be tolerated.' Example 2: Sentence 1: 'Personal data may only be collected, processed, and used for predefined, lawful purposes, and only to the extent necessary to achieve the respective purpose.' Sentence 2: 'Personal data may only be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and may not be further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.').
   - Part-whole relationship through generic terms: An inconsistency exists when both sentences are directed at a specific addressee or a specific situation (subject), but one of the sentences uses a generic term for the other subject or encompasses all subtypes of the other subject (e.g., Sentence 1: 'The processing of personal data is also permissible if government regulations require, presuppose, or permit data processing.'; Sentence 2: 'The processing of employees' personal data is also permissible if government regulations require, presuppose, or permit data processing.').
    
target class: 'Neutral': 
Basically, a sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if a comparison of the two sentences makes sense because they deal with the same topic (the same context) and are neither contradictory nor inconsistent. The target class 'neutral' can be interpreted and classified using the following distinguishing features (important: there are a few exceptions in this category, which are also described below):
   - Neutral words or phrases: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences contains additional neutral words or phrases that do not appear in the other sentence (e.g., sentence 1: 'The road traffic regulations apply throughout the company premises.', sentence 2: 'The road traffic regulations apply on the company premises.', Sentence 1: 'The applicable value according to Module 408.1101 is to be used as the decisive gradient.', Sentence 2: 'In Section 21, Paragraph 1, it has been added that the applicable value of the decisive gradient according to Module 408.1101 must be used.'). However, it is crucial that the words or phrases do not have a restrictive influence on the interpretation or compliance with the respective normative statements, or extend the statements or specifications in such a way that this would result in an inconsistency with the other sentence. In other words, it is possible that a single deviating word can create an inconsistency if it does not appear in the other sentence and thus actually noticeably changes or expands the requirement (e.g., if the word 'in writing' appears in only one of the two sentences! It is therefore important to note that in case of doubt (i.e., if the significance of the supposedly neutral word or phrase is difficult to assess), it is better to choose the target class 'Inconsistent'.
   - Independent normative subordinate clauses: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences contains a subordinate clause that makes an additional normative statement, but one that does not influence, further specify, or concretize the main statement (which the other sentence also addresses), and can instead be considered separately from it. This is typically recognizable by the presence of an additional situation or subject in the sentence (often in combination with another verb). Such independent normative subordinate clauses can appear at the beginning or the end of the main statement (Example 1: Sentence 1: 'The employee ID card must not be given to a third party.' Sentence 2: 'The company ID card must not be transferred to a third party and must be returned upon leaving the company or the enterprise.'; Example 2: Sentence 1: 'All employees are required to use personal protective equipment as intended.'; Sentence 2: 'The contractor must ensure that its employees wear the necessary personal protective equipment.').
   - Use of (additional) examples: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences enriches a normative statement with additional examples, while the other sentence contains no or fewer examples. Such cases must be classified as 'neutral' because the normative statement itself is not changed, but merely explained in more detail by examples of the respective entities/objects (example 1: Sentence 1: 'Work in high places must be secured by protective measures such as parapets or railings.', Sentence 2: 'Workplaces at height must be secured by protective measures such as parapets, safety nets, or railings.', Example 2: Sentence 1: 'The supplier must use preventive maintenance to ensure that the tools, machines, and equipment are functional and ready for use at all times.', Sentence 2: 'Through preventive maintenance (e.g., in accordance with VDI 2890), the supplier shall ensure that the tools, machines, and equipment are functional and ready for use at all times.'). In the second example (in sentence 2), an example was added in parentheses to illustrate how preventive maintenance could be carried out, namely 'in accordance with VDI 2890'. It is important to note that the content of the parentheses is actually mentioned as an example, i.e., if the addition 'e.g.' or 'for example' were missing, the target class 'Inconsistent' would apply instead. The same applies (i.e., you must generally check whether “e.g.” or “for example” precedes the respective additional information) to cases where the corresponding additional information is not mentioned in parentheses.
   - Mentioning an option as an example: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences mentions several possibilities/options for dealing with a situation and the other sentence uses one of these options as an example (e.g., sentence 1: 'If the order to the supplier includes development tasks, the requirements shall be specified in writing by the contracting parties in the form of a specification sheet or technical data sheet (or similar).' , Sentence 2: 'If the order to the supplier includes development tasks, the requirements shall be specified in writing by the contracting parties, e.g. in the form of a specification sheet.'). However, if one of the options is mentioned not as an example but as a fixed instruction, the sentence pair must be classified as a 'contradiction'.
   - Synonyms and abbreviations: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences uses clear synonyms or abbreviations for terms in the other sentence (e.g., sentence 1: 'The FMEA must be continuously updated with regard to errors that have occurred, newly identified risks, defect reports, process changes, etc.' Sentence 2: 'The FMEA must be continuously updated with regard to errors that have occurred, newly identified risks, complaints, process changes, etc.' Sentence 1: 'In the case of standardized surveys, the survey form must be submitted to the DPO for approval.' Sentence 2: 'For standardized surveys, the survey form must be submitted to the data protection officer for approval.'). Important: This rule does not apply to generic terms! Also important: If you are unsure about a potential synonym or abbreviation, it is better to choose the target class 'Inconsistent'.
   - Justifications vs. no justifications: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences provides additional justification or rationale for a normative statement, but the other does not. Such cases can be interpreted as 'neutral' because this additional information is not decisive for compliance with the respective regulation (e.g., Sentence 1: 'Prototypes must be clearly marked with tags and stickers,' Sentence 2: 'Prototypes must be clearly marked with tags and stickers so that they can be distinguished from production parts.').
   - Differing justifications: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if both sentences provide a justification, but these justifications differ. The assumption: As long as the actual normative statement is identical, the justification should be of secondary importance (e.g., Sentence 1: 'If possible, outer packaging/disposable packaging (e.g., films, bags, nonwoven fabrics, etc.) should be avoided in packaging planning to prevent inefficient handling steps,' Statement 2: 'To avoid inefficient handling steps and waste of resources, outer packaging/single-use packaging should be avoided (if possible) during packaging planning.')
   - Potential and actual conditions: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences attaches a (pre)condition to a specific normative statement that is already considered fulfilled in the other sentence (e.g., Sentence 1: 'Based on the deadlines specified by Mustermann AG, the supplier must create a project-specific schedule and make it available to Mustermann AG,' Sentence 2: 'If Mustermann AG specifies deadlines to the supplier, the supplier creates a project-specific schedule and makes it available to Mustermann AG.').
   - Universal statements: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if one of the two sentences makes a normative statement directed at a specific addressee (subject) or a specific situation, while the other sentence expresses the same statement in a completely universal manner, such that it applies to all addressees or situations (Sentence 1: 'All trade and business secrets must be kept confidential both during the term of employment and after its termination.'; Sentence 2: 'Employees must keep all trade and business secrets confidential both during the term of employment and thereafter.'). In any case, however, it is crucial that the sentence which specifically names the addressees/situations does not do so in a strict/restrictive manner (e.g., “Only employees must…”), as this would otherwise contradict the universal statement of the other sentence.
   - Support for locating objects and entities: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if only one of the two sentences specifies where the referenced subjects, objects, or other entities are located in the real world. However, such cases should only be marked as neutral if this information does not alter the respective normative statement but merely provides the reader with additional guidance (e.g., Sentence 1: 'Change requests must be submitted by the supplier in writing using the Product-Change-Notification form,' Sentence 2: 'The supplier must submit change requests in writing using the Product-Change-Notification form (see the Supplier Portal under the “Special Approvals” menu).').
   - Contrasting subject and contrasting object: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if the two sentences have contrasting subject phrases, but the respective object phrases are also contrasting and are therefore 'neutral' in relation to each other (e.g., Sentence 1: 'Mobile scaffolding may only be moved once no one is on it,' Sentence 2: 'Mobile scaffolding must not be moved if there are people on it.'). Keep in mind with this rule that such cases are most likely very rare.
   - Divergent cross-references: A sentence pair is considered 'neutral' if both sentences refer to different places where the (identical) normative statements are found in the respective regulatory document or where more detailed information can be found. This rule is based on our basic assumption that the two sentences provided come from different regulatory documents. This inevitably leads to cases where the sentences refer to different places within their respective documents, simply because the documents may be structured differently (e.g., sentence 1: 'No later than 10 working days before a certificate expires, the holder of this certificate must have provided the successor certificate (see Chapter 9).', Sentence 2: 'The holder of a certificate must have provided the successor certificate at least 10 working days before it expires (see Chapter 7).'). However, as long as the essential normative statement between the two sentences is semantically identical and can stand completely on its own without the respective cross-reference, these cases should be classified as 'neutral'.
    
target class: 'Not related': 
A sentence pair must be classified as 'Not related' if a comparison of the two sentences does not make sense because they do not deal with the same topic. This does not mean that the two sentences are unrelated, but rather that the inherent essential normative statement within the overarching topic is different between the two sentences and therefore they are not related to each other. The target class 'Not related' can be interpreted and classified based on the following distinguishing features:
   - Different subject characteristics: A sentence pair is considered to be a 'Not related' if the essential normative statement (e.g., a requirement/measure/obligation, etc.) is identical in both sentences, but the only subject (situation, entity, addressee, condition, etc.) to which the normative statement refers differs in certain aspects. These cases occur frequently in the regulatory context and especially when comparing different documents, but should not automatically lead to contradiction or inconsistency messages, as both normative statements can refer to several subjects or situations at the same time, i.e., they can apply simultaneously as long as they do not contradict each other. An example of a sentence pair with differing subject characteristics is: Sentence 1: 'The use of heat exchangers made of aluminum materials is not permitted.', Sentence 2: 'The use of heat exchangers made of plastic is not permitted.').
   - (Single) differing verb and no object: A sentence pair is considered to be in a 'Not related' if there is no object in the sentence and the subject or condition is the same in both sentences, but the (only occurring) verb that affects it is different. Since the object is missing, the verb stands as the 'only action' for the respective context in which the sentence is to be interpreted. If these verbs are different, the two sentences are no longer in the same context and can therefore apply simultaneously, i.e., they can be valid at the same time (e.g., sentence 1: 'Compliance with data protection regulations is monitored.', sentence 2: 'Compliance with data protection regulations is logged.').
   - Different contextual nuances: A sentence pair is considered to be 'Not related' if the subject (or situation) and an action relating to the subject are identical, but the context connecting them is different. This would be the case, for example, if two sentences make normative statements about the collection and processing of personal data, but one sentence refers to the 'what for' and the other sentence to the 'how' (e.g., sentence 1: 'Personal data may only be collected and processed for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes.', Sentence 2: 'Personal data must be collected and processed in a lawful manner.').
   - Uncertainty about co-reference: When one subject may be a specified form of the other subject, but this cannot be said with certainty (without knowing the surrounding context). Example: Sentence 1: 'If, in the opinion of the client, measures are necessary, the contractor undertakes to draw up an action plan, implement it in a timely manner, and inform the client thereof.' Sentence 2: 'If, in the opinion of Mustermann AG, measures are necessary, the supplier undertakes to draw up an action plan, implement it in a timely manner, and inform Mustermann AG thereof.' In this example of a sentence pair, it may well be that 'Mustermann AG' stands for 'client' and 'supplier' for 'contractor', but since this cannot be known for certain (at least not without knowing the surrounding context), this sentence pair must be classified as 'Not related'. (Further examples where it cannot be assumed that the same subjects/objects or other entities are involved: 'Mustermann AG' != 'client' | 'Mustermann AG' != 'group of companies' | 'Mustermann AG' != 'company' | 'Mustermann AG' != 'customer' | 'our company' != ' The company' | 'Supplier' != 'Contractor' | 'Supplier' != 'Partner' | 'Supplier' != 'Business partner' | 'Supplier' != 'Contractual partner' | 'Supplier' != 'PAV' | 'You' != 'Contractor' | 'You' != 'Contractor' | 'Employee' != 'Contractor')
    
Important: When making your classification, please take all of the rules described into account at the same time, i.e., always ensure that no other rules or conditions are violated. Feel free to take some time to think about your decision.
Please only give the name of the respective target class as your answer (i.e., either 'Contradiction,' 'Inconsistent,' 'Neutral,' or 'Not related')!
